# Blue Tape Sites Pricing Recommendation

## Bottom line

My recommendation is to **keep pricing on the site, but reframe it**. For Blue Tape Sites, removing prices completely would likely create more friction than advantage because your positioning is built around being clear, direct, and easier to trust than a typical agency. However, the current section should move away from feeling like a fixed menu and toward **investment guidance with context, ROI proof, and a stronger next-step explanation**.

## Should pricing stay on the page?

Yes, but not in its current plain-package form.

Transparent pricing helps serious buyers self-qualify, reduces friction, and builds trust when the prospect wants to know whether they are even in the right ballpark before booking a call.[1] At the same time, public pricing can hurt when premium, customized work is presented without enough context, because prospects may judge on price before they understand scope, outcomes, or return.[2]

That tradeoff matters for Blue Tape Sites. Your ICP feedback does **not** read like people are offended by pricing. It reads like they want more proof that the price leads to money, more clarity about what happens next, and more trust signals around the people behind the company. In other words, the issue is not "you showed the number"; the issue is "you have not yet fully justified the number."

## What other agencies commonly do

The most common pattern is not a hard binary between "full pricing" and "no pricing." Many agencies use one of three approaches.

| Approach | What it looks like | Best use case | Main risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| No public pricing | "Contact us for a custom quote" | Highly custom enterprise projects | Creates friction and weakens self-qualification |
| Starting prices or ranges | "Projects start at $3,000" or "Typical engagements are $3,000-$8,000" | Services with some repeatable scope and some customization | Needs context so buyers do not oversimplify scope |
| Tiered packages with qualifiers | Clear packages plus notes on who each is for, what results it targets, and when scope expands | Productized or semi-standardized service offers | Can feel commodity-like if proof and positioning are thin |

For Blue Tape Sites, the strongest fit is the **second and third approach combined**: keep visible entry pricing, but present it as **starting investment tiers tied to outcomes and business stage**, not as rigid off-the-shelf web design boxes.

## My direct answer: could public pricing hinder clients?

Yes, it can hinder some prospects, but probably not the prospects you most want.

If someone wants a premium result but refuses to see numbers until a sales call, that is not automatically bad. But your ICP comments suggest a different psychology: they want confidence, specificity, and proof. Hiding pricing would likely increase mystery at the exact moment they are asking for more certainty.

The bigger risk is **under-explained pricing**, not visible pricing. If the page shows dollar amounts without proof, timeline, process, or expected commercial upside, some better-fit prospects may anchor on cost too early. If the same page shows ROI evidence, a sample audit, exact next steps, and more founder credibility, pricing becomes a trust asset rather than a liability.

## Recommended pricing change

I would change the section from **Pricing Packages** to an **Investment** section with this structure:

| Recommended change | Why it helps |
| --- | --- |
| Rename pricing to "Investment" or "Ways to work together" | Feels less commodity-like while staying transparent |
| Change hard package framing to "starting at" or "typical range" language where appropriate | Preserves flexibility for custom scope |
| Add one-line fit guidance under each tier | Helps prospects self-sort by company stage and urgency |
| Add one short ROI-style proof line near pricing | Connects spend to revenue, leads, or booked work |
| Add exact audit follow-up steps beneath the CTA | Removes uncertainty after form submission |
| Expand retainer bullets into clear deliverables | Makes recurring spend feel concrete and safer |

A good directional example would be: "Homepage rebuilds typically start at $X. Most redesign engagements land between $Y and $Z depending on page count, copy depth, and service-area strategy." That keeps transparency while protecting you from being boxed into a flat quote.

## Highest-priority ICP-driven improvements

Based on the comments you shared, these are the most valuable homepage changes in order.

| Priority | Improvement | Why it matters most |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Add 2 to 3 hard-number case studies | This is the clearest missing bridge between price and ROI |
| 2 | Clarify exactly what happens after the audit request | Removes uncertainty and makes the CTA safer to click |
| 3 | Show a clearer audit preview, ideally with one sharper sample and annotations | Helps prospects visualize the deliverable, not just imagine it |
| 4 | Add a short founder/local credibility section about Luke and Fullerton | Increases trust for a newer company with local-buyer psychology |
| 5 | Strengthen the retainer section with exact monthly deliverables | Makes recurring pricing easier to accept |
| 6 | Add one short walkthrough video | Likely raises trust and comprehension quickly for mobile and truck-time viewing |
| 7 | Add more testimonial or review proof | Supports the newer-brand credibility gap |

## Final recommendation

Do **not** remove pricing entirely. Instead, make pricing more premium and more persuasive by surrounding it with proof, scope context, and a clearer process.

If you want Blue Tape Sites to stand out, transparent pricing can absolutely be part of that difference. The right move is not to become mysterious like every other agency. The right move is to be **clearer than other agencies and more credible than your current version**.
