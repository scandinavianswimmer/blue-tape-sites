# Website Teardown Workflow

**Purpose:** Standardize how Blue Tape Sites qualifies prospects and produces short teardown notes for outbound outreach.

## Why This Workflow Exists

The first outbound sprint should not rely on intuition alone. A repeatable teardown workflow helps Blue Tape Sites choose better-fit prospects, spot the most common website problems quickly, and write outreach that feels specific instead of generic. The goal is not to produce full audits for free. The goal is to generate just enough sharp insight to earn a reply.

## Qualification Rules

A company should only make the first-50 sheet if it matches both the niche and the business-quality threshold.

| Category | Include When | Exclude When |
|---|---|---|
| **Vertical** | The company is clearly a plumber or remodeler | The company sits outside the current sprint niches |
| **Geography** | Southern California or a nearby priority market | Broad national lists with no clear focus |
| **Business reality** | The company appears active, legitimate, and visibly operating | The company appears inactive, extremely small, or abandoned |
| **Website problem** | The site has real trust, clarity, UX, or presentation gaps | The site is already strong enough that outreach would feel forced |
| **Budget likelihood** | The company appears established enough to invest in its presentation | The company appears price-first with little interest in quality or brand |

## Scoring Model

Every prospect should receive two scores before outreach begins.

| Score | Range | What It Means |
|---|---:|---|
| **Website Quality Score** | 1–10 | How strong the current website is overall |
| **Fit Score** | 1–10 | How promising the company is as a Blue Tape Sites client |

A lower website-quality score is not automatically better. Blue Tape Sites wants companies with visible website problems **and** signs of being worth helping. A neglected but obviously active company is better than a very polished company with little need. Likewise, an extremely weak site attached to an obviously tiny or low-commitment business may be a poor fit.

## Teardown Categories

The next agent should review each prospect through the same five lenses.

| Category | What to Look For | Example Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Mobile trust** | Does the site feel credible and usable on a phone? | “Hero feels cramped and phone CTA is not obvious on first screen.” |
| **Offer clarity** | Is it instantly clear what they do and why to choose them? | “Headline is generic and does not explain specialty or differentiator.” |
| **Proof** | Are there believable trust signals? | “Testimonials are weak and there is no named review or project evidence.” |
| **Call path** | Is the next action obvious and low-friction? | “Quote path is buried and the most important CTA gets lost.” |
| **Presentation quality** | Does the site match the quality of the business? | “Business looks established, but the design makes it feel cheaper than it is.” |

## Teardown Output Format

The outreach note should never include more than three problems. The best teardown is short, concrete, and clearly useful.

| Field | Rule |
|---|---|
| **Primary issue 1** | Strongest trust or clarity problem |
| **Primary issue 2** | Second strongest problem, ideally different in type |
| **Primary issue 3** | Optional, only if it sharpens the pattern |
| **Message angle** | One dominant angle only |

## Recommended Message Angles by Niche

| Niche | Good Message Angles |
|---|---|
| **Plumber** | Mobile trust, fast calls, proof, CTA clarity, emergency confidence |
| **Remodeler** | Premium presentation, gallery quality, trust, qualification, project-storytelling |

## Step-by-Step Workflow

| Step | Action | Output |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Visit the company website and confirm it matches the niche | Keep or discard the prospect |
| **2** | Check mobile presentation and first-screen clarity | Note the strongest trust issue |
| **3** | Review proof, CTA path, and overall presentation | Note one or two more issues |
| **4** | Assign website-quality and fit scores | Prioritize A or B tier |
| **5** | Choose one message angle only | Keeps outreach focused |
| **6** | Write a short teardown note in plain English | Ready for outreach |

## Good Teardown Example

> The business looks established, but the site is not carrying the same confidence yet. On mobile, the first screen does not make the next step obvious, the trust proof is thinner than it should be, and the overall layout makes the company feel smaller than it likely is in real life.

## Bad Teardown Example

> Your SEO is poor, the branding is off, the UI needs work, your structure is confusing, the fonts are weak, your navigation is not ideal, and your messaging could be improved.

The bad example fails because it sounds generic, overloaded, and low-value.

## Final Rule

A good teardown should make the prospect feel two things at once: first, that Blue Tape Sites noticed something real; second, that the fix is possible and worth a conversation.
